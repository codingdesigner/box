What are we designin’?

  • Not web pages
  • Design Systems
  • Small and Large Scale
**Not web pages**. We're **designing systems**. We need to create a design language that will not just serve the project we're delivering, but will continue to carry the weight of new content. We need to think in both the **small and the large scale**.

This Leads Us to Style Guides

A traditional product of creating a design system is for the designer to create a style guide. This might be done after a design is complete as a way to communicate that design thinking to other stakeholders or future designers.

Our design language needs to sustain a feeling and character across many different sections of content. If the brand is cool and sophisticated it probably won't do to lose sight of that and start adding tattoo flames. That's why we have style guides. They codify a design so it can be communicated to others. Recently major brands have begun **publishing html style guides publicly**. This makes it easy for new designers to create new work for them while maintaining the brand. It also helps us grow as an industry if we can see and learn from each other.

Style Guide as Design Tool

  • Atomic Design
  • Pattern Lab

I'm suggesting that we flip this process and start with the style guide and treat it like a **design tool**. I'm certainly not the first. Brad Frost has written about this extensively. You may have seen him write about the concept of **atomic design**. He proposes starting with the smallest things you can design, calling them atoms, and working your way up through bigger and bigger constructs until you've designed full templates and pages. At Phase2 we've been using **Pattern Lab**, his styleguide creation tool on many of our projects. We'll be looking at that later.

By starting with the style guide and creating the reference for the design language as we create it we get a very valuable perspective that allows us to fine tune the minute details and see how all the little pieces combine to create the whole.

We’re Talkin’ About Component-Driven Design

  • self-contained chunk of design
  • extension of well-formed content
  • assembled into other components and combined into layouts
A bit more of a direct way to look at this is to see that each piece, whether it's an atom, a molecule, a menu, an image gallery, an author byline, or whatever is a Component. A design component is a **self-contained chunk of design and is an extension of well-formed content**. And components can be assembled into other components and combined into layouts. So instead of designing a page the goal is to design an interlocking family of components that become our design language.

Blank Page vs Style Guide

When we start with an **empty style guide** instead of an empty page we actually have a lot less emptyness. Of course you can start anywhere, but I think there are many better places to start than your favorite image editor.

Organize Content

When we start with basic markup in html we get a **scaffolding** where we can **create our design language**, understand and **organize our content**. This is the raw result of the content modelling part of our content strategy. It's super helpful because when we start with the right content definitions it asks us the right questions about how we should design for it.

Design Iterations

Then do the hard work of **going over and over it as a designer** until it has a consistent tone. How should these elements lay out on small and large devices? Is the design language conveying the right tone?

Assemble Components

As we develop our design language we'll also combine the smaller components into more fleshed out layouts. By building it up from scratch and using the **style guide to guide our styles** it's much easier to spot patterns, redundancies, and inconsitencies and add intentional contrast and surprise.

Hard Bop

  • Melody
  • Tonality
  • Chords
  • Form

This approach to designing strikes me as similar to how **hard bop** Jazz is stereotypically structured. We all know that there’s a lot of improvisation in Jazz, but a song doesn’t come from nowhere, and it doesn’t all come out at once.

  • A composer creates the **tune**
  • in a key, creates a **tonality** and
  • **chord progression**, and
  • defines the song's **form**.

That’s what the composer writes down. That’s the song as a document – the style guide for the song. Then he’ll take that to the band and they’ll play that tune once. It usually takes less than a minute, but the band continues playing. This is where the improvisation kicks in.

What’s going on is that players will take turns soloing over all the source material the composer gave them. The players mine the song form and chord progression for interesting material. It’s not a free-for-all. They’re not trying to play any different songs. They’re exploiting the vocabulary the composer gave them. Sounds like design to me.

Let's think about that in tems of **content-first design**.

Good music is not good because it's in 4/4, or 5/8, or resolves to a D Locrian with a sharp 9. Although that's pretty cool. It's all about the core melody and lyrics. The content. So let's start with that as we design.

We should know what our content is going to be **before we try to decorate** it. We'll probably never have every word and image in advance, but we can do a thorough **content inventory and modelling** and be able to map out the characteristics of the content. That's what we need in order to make our CMS after all, and it's no different for our design.

By starting with the content we have the opportunity to **follow its lead**. It's the first piece of our design vocabulary.

Component Second Design

  • Look for patterns
  • Combine into bigger components
  • Assemble into templates

Don't just string all that content out, light a candle and hope for the best. In musical terms you can't have variations without a theme. Melodies need structure and context, and your content is no different.

Mold it into various components that enhance the tone and message of the content. Is it verbose or pithy? All about the hard sell? Image-heavy? **Look for patterns** to emerge and reinforce each other. Combine and reorder the components into **bigger components**. **Assemble them into page templates**. We don't have any decoration, or even color yet, but if we do this right by our content it will do right by us and help keep the design on track.

Is this a small band or a Big Band, or even a rock band? Any of these could play our song, but they'd all do it differently. We need a direction.

Time to start adding the basic graphic elements of the design. What can we do first? I usually start with **typefaces**, **sizes**, and **colors**. I've found that you can go a long way towards defining a design language by finding the right combination of these elements.

Everything you create in your design will use these elements so it's worth the time up front to get them right.

Finding the Right Type Sizes

Sassy Modular Scale

  • Use ratios to establish sizes
  • Set of Limitations
modularscale.com - github.com/at-import/modular-scale

Once I decide on a font face I like to find a set of font sizes that work harmoniously.

Let's look at an example. THE GREAT DISCONTENT

**Modular Scale** uses a concept that's been in typographic practice for hundreds of years and uses consistent ratios to establish relationships between type sizes. In fact the ratios are often expressed as musical intervals. This is a good time to do some experimenting.

For that, my friend Scott Kellum and I wrote Sassy Modular Scale to bring Tim Brown's Modular Scale concept to Sass.

In the **Sass version** you have a lot of control to create a scale with a lot of wide jumps in size or something more tightly spaced. The sizes you choose should show the type at its best, and also help to give your design language a certain character. It establishes a **set of limitations**. Just keep in mind that this is very similar to a musical scale. You don't have to always play the notes in order.

Finding the Right Colors

Color Schemer

  • Single Seed Color
  • Harmonious Color Families
  • Experiment and Tweak
github.com/at-import/color-schemer
Another Sass tool we can thank Scott Kellum for is **Color Schemer**. This gives us some very powerful color theory tools in Sass. This lets you use a **single seed color** to generate **harmonious color families**. This is similar to tools like Kuler and Color Scheme Designer that help you use analagous and complimentary colors from the color wheel. Since this is Sass you can programatically experiment and tweak until you're happy.

Working with Teams and Clients

Unity

  • splitting out the components
  • designers can work simultaneously
  • integrate photoshop-based designers
  • pair with front end developers

These are design iterations. What's great about working with this process is that I've found it to be helpful for working as a solo designer on a project and as part of a larger team. By **splitting out the components** intentionally different **designers can work simultaneously** and still be aware of how the design is changing in shape and form.

You can also **integrate photoshop-based designers** by asking them to submit components to the style guide and having a **front end developer do them in css** early. This gives everyone a chance to experiment while also benefiting from the rapid prototyping process.

Pattern Lab

sassconf.com SassConf Styleguide
I've been using Pattern lab as an example here so I want to give you a brief tour of some of the things I like about it. Pattern Lab is centered arround giving you a low-friction way to define whatever design components you need, organize them into whatever categories or groupings you like, assemble them into bigger components, and then templates, and finally pages. You can add whatever CSS and JavaScript you want. You don't have to use Sass (altho *really?*) and you're really free to use any front end technique you like. One of my favourite bits of the UI is that it lets you quickly drag the width slider to see how the design works at different breakpoints. For me it's really about enabling the design iterations to go quickly and to give you clarity on the global shape of the design language as it progresses.

github.com/Snugug/eq.js
My friends Scott Kellum and Sam Richard made my favorite grid system. Using Sass variables and mixins you can define any grid you like. You are no longer limited to the tyranny of the equal column. Assymetric or arbitrary grids are still grids. Your columns gan be any widths you want. You can define a grid and use it on any element. You can use a totally different grid at different breakpoints. While you can have one, you definitely don't need a master grid for all your layouts, and you can define multiple master grids, as well as mini grids for components as you need them.

github.com/at-import/breakpoint
We end up writing a lot of media queries in responsive design. Breakpoint is a Sass tool I created with Sam Richard for simplifying and organizing media queries. You can assign a media query to a **variable name** using an intuitive **shorthand syntax**. This makes it very easy to experiment with responsive layouts that may be used in many different places in your project.

Element Queries

eq.js

  • Not Native To Css
  • Add States As A Data-attribute
  • Style Based On Data Attributes
github.com/Snugug/eq.js
Unfortnuately we **don't have element queries in css**. These would allow us to alter the styling of an element if the container that it's in changes dimensions. Fortunately my friend Sam Richard has written a lightweight javascript implementation called eq.js. It works by setting **data attributes** to an element under different conditions.

Learn one simple trick to keep your styleguide alive from design through launch and beyond.
